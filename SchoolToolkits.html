School Toolkits

The interviews conducted throughout the course of this community needs assessment with school officials, including counselors, teachers and other faculty, suggest that there is not consistent awareness of the symptoms of food insecurity in children or what options are available for food insecure families. In one area surveyed, twenty out of thirty eight pantry visitors with children stated that the reason they did not utilize free summer feeding was because they didn't know about the program. In addition to this, school faculty frequently expressed frustration over not knowing what options are available to them for addressing food insecurity within their schools.

My recommendation for starting to address this issue is to develop two toolkits for distribution in schools, beginning with schools in areas estimated to have a high rate of food insecurity.

The first of these two toolkits would be written to be distributed to school faculty, mainly teachers and counselors. This toolkit would include information about some of the signs of food insecurity in children, as well and what sorts of long term health and developmental impacts periods of food insecurity can have on children. The toolkit would include the two food insecurity screening questions, which could be asked by teachers in parent-teacher conferences, or other private meetings with parents or guardians, and include information about next steps if they receive a positive response.

The second toolkit would be written to be distributed by school faculty to parents or guardians, and be more along the lines of a list of resources and how to access them, as well as include some information about how to safely and healthily stretch a food budget. This could be customized for each school with information about which pantries are closest, who to call for a pantry referral or to begin a SNAP application, closest summer feeding sites, school counselor information, or any other location dependent information.

Ideally, the distribution of these resources would be paired with an in-person presentation from a member of Food Bank staff, in order to explain why this resource is being provided, ensure the toolkits are not glossed over by administration, present compelling information on the impacts of child hunger, and to answer any questions that might arise.
